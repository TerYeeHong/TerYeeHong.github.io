Digital Illustration

For Beginners

by Yee Hong

What is Digital Art?

The earliest use of art done using a digital software dates back to the 1980s. Initially it was not well received as the concept was still very foreign. Now, with the rise of technology and much more advanced softwares, Digital Art is becoming the norm in big creative industries.

Vector Art Style

Vector artwork is art that's made up of vector graphics. These graphics are points, lines, curves and shapes that are based on mathematical formulas.

When you scale a vector image file, it isn't low resolution and there's no loss of quality, so it can be sized to however large or small you need it to be.

It's an excellent tool for putting company logos on business cards, creating poster designs, and when photo-shopping in Adobe Photoshop.

Pixel Art Style

Pixel art is a form of digital art drawn with graphical software where images are built using pixels as the only building block.

Fun fact: the word "pixel" was formed from the words "picture" and "element"

Digital Illustration

Digital illustration is the process of creating images using a computer. It encompasses a wide range of mediums and techniques, from traditional drawing and painting to 3D rendering and animation.

Illustration Process

Step 1:
Rough Sketch

Use guiding lines and basic shapes such as circles to form the pose and figure of the character.

At this point, you do not need to draw in any details.

Step 2:
Proper Sketch

Using a different layer and darker brush, sketch out the pose again and add in details such as facial features and clothing.

Step 3:
Outline

Make another layer and use your darkest brush to now smoothly outline the painting. Clean up and refine lines that may be uneven or messy.

Make sure all lines are connected and there is no gap.

Step 4:
Apply Base Colour

Using the Outline, fill in the areas with a base colour, no mixing or blending of colours yet.

Paint tools are one of the best ways to do so.

Step 5:
Shading

This is now the final and usually the most difficult step of all. Create the effect of shadows and lights.

This step can often be split into more steps, eg. Soft Shadows, Hard Shadows, Lighting...

Some useful techniques to help with blending are blending modes.

Techniques

Before you get started, let me share some tips and tricks that I have learnt over the years. I will share with you about Blending Modes and Colour Theory.

Blending Modes

Blending modes are very useful tools to help you do the last step of illustrations which is applying shadows and lighting. Here is a very informative video about it which I will reference from.

Important side note! Each layer can only use 1 blending mode. Create a new layer before you apply a blending mode.

Blending mode can interact with all other layers to create a blend without actually altering the pixels themselves.

Blending Mode Categories

  1. DARKEN
  2. LIGHTEN
  3. CONTRAST
  4. INVERSION
  5. COLOR

Multiply - Linear Burn - Color Burn - Darken - Darken Color

DARKEN

This Catergory of blending mode are used for shading.

They blend with pixels from other layers to create a darken shade of those pixels.

Multiply is used commonly, Linear Burn is a stronger shading version of Multiply.

Screen - Linear Dodge - Color Dodge - Lighten - Lighter Color

LIGHTEN

This Catergory of blending mode are used for lighting.

They blend with pixels from other layers to create a lighten shade of those pixels.

Its like the opposite of the Darken blending mode.

Screen is used commonly, Linear Dodge is a stronger version of Multiply.

Overlay - Soft Light - Hard Light - Vivid Light - Linear Light - Pin Light - Hard Mix

CONTRAST

This Blending Mode is a combination of Darken and Lighten

In layman terms, light colours become lighter, dark colours become darker.

Overlay is Multiply + Screen.

Difference - Exclusion - Subtract - Divide

INVERSION

This Catergory of blending mode is used for inverting colours.

Generally hard to be useful in Digital art. Its a more abstract tool to use.

Hue - Color - Luminosity - Saturation

COLOR

This Catergory of blending mode changes the base color.

As their name suggests, they each change that different aspect of the pixel.

Colour Theory

When making a design, try to use a Colour Scheme and Colour Temperature for Design Harmony.

When finding Colour Schemes, start with taking reference to a colour wheel.

Colour Schemes

Monochromatic

Take one hue and create other elements from different shades and tints of it.

Analogous

Use three colours located beside one another on the colour wheel (e.g, orange, yellow-orange and yellow to show sunlight).

Complementary

Use "opposite colours" pairs - e.g, blue/yellow - to maximize contrast.

Triadic

Take three colours which are equally distant on the colour wheel (120 degrees apart: e.g, red/blue/yellow).

These colours may not be vibrant, but the scheme can be as it maintains harmony and high contrast.

It's easier to make visually appealing designs with this than with a complementary scheme.

How to Practice?

Now that you know about the tips and tricks, the steps to draw a illustration.

You may wonder, what are the best ways to practice to improve the fastest?

Let me share with you 2 advices if you truly want to see the fastest growth.

1. Copy from famous works

Copying or using artworks as references can help you learn and apply the techniques.

Drawing the same shapes and line helps train your muscle memory for anatomy.

If there is a time-lapse or live stream of the artist doing the work, following along can provide the best way to learn.


Now I get what some people may be thinking; Isn't copying others bad? or downright unlawful?

First, copying someone's work and publishing it as your own or using it for monetary purposes is wrong.

But to copy or reference for the sake of practice is not wrong at all.

Also, some people wants to develop their own style and hence choose not to reference any artist at all.

My advice is learn from professionals first, do exactly as they do, once you are more skillful, then expand and develop your own style.

2. Consistency

Like with any skill, consistent practice is the key to mastery.

Having a drawing session every week will help you improve much faster than a super intense drawing session once every few months.

During practice, you don't have to complete a full artwork everytime.

If you are too busy for a full drawing session every week, a short sketching of poses while you're on the train or anywhere is still useful.

Focus on an area before moving to the next. Practice the area you are weak at repeatedly instead of constantly changing focus areas and learning new art styles.
